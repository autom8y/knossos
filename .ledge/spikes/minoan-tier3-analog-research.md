# Minoan-Period Analog Research for Tier 3 Names

## Question and Context

The mythology-naming alignment initiative established a four-tier naming provenance framework. Four platform names are currently classified as **Tier 3 (Hellenistic/Scholarly)**, meaning they were borrowed from post-classical scholarship or Panhellenic practice rather than from the Knossos myth cycle or Bronze Age attestation. This spike investigates whether closer Minoan-period (Late Bronze Age) analogs exist in the Linear B corpus that would allow any of these names to be reclassified to Tier 1 or Tier 2, strengthening the platform's mythological coherence.

**Decision this informs**: Whether to KEEP these Tier 3 names as-is, MIGRATE them to Linear B attested alternatives, or DEFER pending further scholarship.

**Key references consulted**:
- Ventris & Chadwick, *Documents in Mycenaean Greek* (1956, 2nd ed. 1973)

## 1. Theoria

### Current Derivation

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Name** | Theoria |
| **Tier** | 3 (Hellenistic/Scholarly) |
| **Source** | Panhellenic practice (Herodotus, Thucydides) |
| **Period** | Classical Greek (5th century BCE) |
| **Semantic field** | Sacred state delegation of observers; structured contemplation |
| **Platform role** | Audit operation -- structured delegation of observers dispatched to assess domain health |

### Minoan-Period Investigation

**Etymology**: Greek *theoria* derives from *theoros* ("one who sees the divine" or "spectator"), itself from *thea* ("a viewing") + *horan* ("to see"). The root *the-* connects to *theos* (god), which IS attested in Linear B as **te-o** (/theos/, "god/divine"). Linear B tablets from Knossos record offerings to *te-o* in religious contexts (e.g., KN Fp 1+31, KN V 52).

**The gap**: While the component *te-o* (god/divine) is Bronze Age attested, the compound *theoria* as a concept -- "structured observation of the divine" or "sacred delegation" -- is NOT attested in Linear B. The Mycenaean palace administration recorded offerings, personnel, and inventories, but the concept of dispatching an official observation delegation appears to be a later development of the Greek polis system (post-palatial, Classical period).

**Closest Mycenaean analogs investigated**:
- **te-o-jo do-e-ro** (KN tablets): "servants of the god" -- religious personnel, not observers
- **i-je-re-u** / **i-je-re-ja** (priest/priestess): religious officials, not observer-delegates
- No Linear B term for "one who is sent to observe" has been identified in the corpus

**Scholarly consensus**: The institution of theoria as a formal state delegation is a product of the Greek polis period (Archaic-Classical, 8th-5th c. BCE). Mycenaean palace religion operated through a different organizational model -- palace-administered cult with designated religious personnel rather than external delegations. Rutherford (2013, *State Pilgrims and Sacred Observers in Ancient Greece*) traces theoria as an institution no earlier than the Archaic period.

### Recommendation: **KEEP**

**Rationale**: The platform's use of Theoria is architecturally precise -- it describes a structured observation delegation, which is a Classical Greek institutional form with no Bronze Age predecessor. The doctrine already acknowledges this honestly: "these are borrowed from the wider Greek tradition because they describe their architectural functions with precision no Cretan alternative could match." The component *te-o* being Bronze Age attested does not make the compound institution Bronze Age. Forcing a Tier 1 analog here would sacrifice semantic precision for false provenance.

## 2. Pinakes

### Current Derivation

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Name** | Pinakes |
| **Tier** | 3 (Hellenistic/Scholarly) |
| **Source** | Callimachus of Cyrene (~250 BCE), Library of Alexandria |
| **Period** | Hellenistic (3rd century BCE) |
| **Semantic field** | Systematic catalog/classification of knowledge; "tablets" or "boards" (plural of *pinax*) |
| **Platform role** | Domain registry legomena -- catalogs audit targets, evaluation criteria, grading rubrics |

### Minoan-Period Investigation

**Etymology**: Greek *pinax* (plural *pinakes*) literally means "board, plank, tablet." The word itself is pre-Hellenistic and appears in Homer (*Iliad* 6.169: the "sema lugra" written on a folded *pinax*), but its use as a systematic catalog is Callimachus's innovation.

**Linear B tablet classification systems**: The Mycenaean scribes at Knossos did employ a sophisticated classification system for their tablets. Tablets were organized into **series** designated by letter combinations (e.g., KN Fp = oil offerings, KN Sc = personnel, KN Da = livestock). These series were determined by the dominant ideogram on the tablet and served as a de facto catalog system. However:

1. The series designators (Fp, Sc, Da, etc.) are **modern scholarly conventions** assigned by Evans, Bennett, and Chadwick during excavation and classification -- not Mycenaean self-designations.
2. No Linear B word meaning "catalog," "index," or "systematic list of lists" has been identified.
3. The physical form -- clay tablets (*de-to* / *deltos* is NOT attested in Linear B; the tablets were utilitarian recording media, not conceptualized as a meta-catalog system).

**Closest Mycenaean analogs investigated**:
- **to-pe-za** (/torpeza/, "table"): attested in Linear B (PY Ta 641), but refers to physical furniture, not a conceptual catalog
- The Linear B corpus shows no evidence of meta-organization -- no "tablet about tablets," no index of series, no self-referential classification system
- The KN series designators represent sophisticated administrative practice but are implicit in the physical organization of tablet storage (findspots in the palace), not in any named concept

**Scholarly consensus**: Mycenaean record-keeping was sophisticated but annually transient -- tablets were typically annual records remade each administrative year (Palaima 2004). The concept of a permanent, systematic catalog of knowledge (which is what Pinakes means in the Knossos platform) is an intellectual innovation of the Hellenistic library tradition. Casson (2001, *Libraries in the Ancient World*) confirms the Pinakes as a uniquely Alexandrian achievement.

### Recommendation: **KEEP**

**Rationale**: The platform's Pinakes names the specific concept of "a systematic meta-catalog that describes what domains exist and how to assess them" -- this is a self-referential classification function. The Mycenaean scribal system classified tablets by content but never created a tablet about the classification itself. The Hellenistic origin is semantically correct: Callimachus invented the concept the platform is implementing. No Bronze Age term captures this meta-cataloging function. The doctrine's provenance tier is honest and accurate.

## 3. Synkrisis

### Current Derivation

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Name** | Synkrisis |
| **Tier** | 3 (Hellenistic/Scholarly) |
| **Source** | Plutarch, *Parallel Lives* (1st-2nd century CE) |
| **Period** | Imperial Roman/Late Classical (Plutarch: c. 46-120 CE) |
| **Semantic field** | Structured comparison; cross-domain pattern synthesis |
| **Platform role** | Synthesis step -- weaves individual theoros reports into cross-domain patterns |

### Minoan-Period Investigation

**Etymology**: Greek *synkrisis* derives from *syn-* ("together") + *krinein* ("to judge, separate, decide"). The root *krinein* is PIE \**krey-* ("to sieve, separate"). Neither *syn-* nor *krin-* has confirmed Linear B attestation as a compound, though both roots are old.

**Mycenaean accounting and comparison**: The Linear B tablets DO show evidence of comparative/differential recording practices, though not under a named concept:

1. **o-pe-ro** (/ophelos/, "deficit/debt"): Attested across multiple tablet series (PY Ma, PY Cn, etc.). This term marks quantities owed or outstanding -- the gap between what was expected and what was delivered. It demonstrates the Mycenaean administration could compute and record differences.

2. **a-pu-do-si** (/apudosis/, "delivery/payment"): Attested in Linear B, recording what was actually delivered against obligations. The pairing of a-pu-do-si with o-pe-ro across tablet series shows a proto-comparative accounting practice: expected vs. actual, delivered vs. owed.

3. **Ideogram O**: Used as an abbreviation for "deficit" in accounting notation, showing a standardized way of flagging discrepancies.

**The gap**: While the Mycenaean administration practiced comparison at the operational level (what was owed vs. what was paid), this was **quantitative accounting comparison**, not the **qualitative synthesis** that Synkrisis names in the platform. Plutarch's synkrisis compared lives -- extracting patterns, contrasts, and structural insights from parallel narratives. The platform's Synkrisis similarly synthesizes qualitative domain assessments, not numerical ledgers.

**Closest Mycenaean analogs investigated**:
- **o-pe-ro** (/ophelos/): semantically "deficit" -- a comparison result, not the act of comparison itself
- No Linear B term for "comparison," "juxtaposition," or "synthesis" has been identified
- The scribal practice of deficit-tracking is operationally comparative but conceptually narrower than synkrisis

### Recommendation: **KEEP**

**Rationale**: The Mycenaean accounting system demonstrates proto-comparative practices (deficit tracking via o-pe-ro, delivery verification via a-pu-do-si), but these are quantitative ledger operations, not the qualitative cross-domain synthesis that Synkrisis names in the platform. Migrating to "o-pe-ro" would be a false equivalence: ophelos means "what is owed," not "what is revealed by comparison." Plutarch's synkrisis remains the precise analog for the platform's synthesis operation. The Tier 3 classification is correct.

**Note for doctrine**: The existence of o-pe-ro and a-pu-do-si as Bronze Age comparative accounting terms is interesting scholarship that could enrich the Synkrisis doctrine section (acknowledging that the Mycenaean administration practiced comparison, even if they did not name it as a concept), but it does not warrant a name migration.

## 4. Argus

### Current Derivation

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Name** | Argus (Argus Panoptes) |
| **Tier** | 2 (Classical Source) |
| **Source** | Apollodorus, Ovid (Argive myth cycle) |
| **Period** | Classical/Hellenistic literary tradition |
| **Semantic field** | Total vigilance through distributed observation; the hundred-eyed guardian |
| **Platform role** | N-agent parallel dispatch pattern -- one body (main thread), many eyes (agents) |

**Note**: The concordance already classifies Argus as Tier 2, not Tier 3. The track spec lists it as a Tier 3 investigation target, so we investigate regardless.

### Minoan-Period Investigation

**Etymology**: Greek *Argos* likely derives from *argos* ("bright, shining, white") from PIE \**h2erg-* ("to shine"). The name Argos/Argus is deeply embedded in Greek tradition -- the city of Argos, the Argo (Jason's ship), Odysseus's dog Argos. None of these are Linear B attested in the sense of "the hundred-eyed watcher."

**Mycenaean watchfulness terminology**: The Linear B corpus contains SIGNIFICANT evidence of organized surveillance and watchfulness in the palace administration:

1. **o-ka** tablets (PY An 654, An 657, An 661): Record approximately 800 "coast watchers" organized into 10 units of 10-110 men. The o-ka (likely /orkha/, "command/detachment") tablets from Pylos are the most extensive military/surveillance records in the Linear B corpus.

2. **e-pi-ko-wo** (/epikorwoi/ or /epikowoi/): Attested on the Pylos o-ka tablets. Interpreted as auxiliary guards or "those stationed alongside warriors." Scholarly debate exists: earlier interpretations connected to "fire-watchers" (cf. Hesychian gloss *pyrkoooi*); modern consensus (e.g., Palaima, Deger-Jalkotzy) favors "auxiliary/accompanying personnel." Either way, these are guards with a watching function.

3. **pu-ko-wo** (cf. **pyrkoooi**, "fire-watchers"): Possibly attested, connected to Hesychian gloss on fire-watchers at Delphi. If the identification holds, these are literal watchers -- guards who maintained fire-watch over the palace. However, the reading and interpretation remain disputed.

4. The Pylos "watchers of the sea" represent the most vivid Bronze Age surveillance system in the Greek record: a distributed observation network with multiple units covering different coastal sectors -- architecturally analogous to the Argus Pattern (one command, many distributed observation posts).

**The crucial distinction**: The platform's Argus Pattern names a *technique* (N-agent parallel dispatch), not a *role* (watchman). The Linear B evidence attests to roles (coast watchers, fire-watchers, auxiliary guards) but not to a named pattern or technique of distributed observation. The Argus myth provides the conceptual frame -- "one body, many eyes" -- that no Mycenaean administrative term captures.

**Closest Mycenaean analogs investigated**:
- **o-ka** (/orkha/): "command/detachment" -- the organizational unit, not the observation pattern
- **e-pi-ko-wo**: auxiliary guards -- a role, not a pattern
- **pu-ko-wo**: possibly "fire-watchers" -- a role, not a pattern
- No Linear B term for "distributed watching" or "total observation" as an abstract concept has been identified

### Recommendation: **KEEP**

**Rationale**: The platform uses "Argus Pattern" to name an abstract architectural technique (parallel dispatch for distributed observation), not a specific watchman role. While the Linear B corpus attests to organized watching practices -- and the Pylos o-ka tablets describe a distributed coastal surveillance network that is operationally analogous to the Argus Pattern -- no Mycenaean term names this as an abstraction. The myth of Argus Panoptes provides precisely the conceptual metaphor needed: one entity with many simultaneous eyes. Migrating to "o-ka Pattern" or "e-pi-ko-wo Pattern" would trade a vivid conceptual metaphor for an administrative term that describes the wrong semantic level (organizational unit vs. architectural pattern).

**Note for doctrine**: The concordance already classifies Argus as Tier 2 (Classical Source), which is correct. The Argive myth cycle (Apollodorus, Ovid) is the source. The track spec's classification of Argus as Tier 3 appears to be an error in the spec; the concordance is authoritative.

**Note for enrichment**: The o-ka tablets and their distributed coastal surveillance system could be cited in the doctrine's Argus Pattern section as a real-world Bronze Age operational precedent for the pattern the myth encodes. The Mycenaean administration *practiced* the Argus Pattern without naming it.

## Key Findings

1. **All four names should be KEPT at their current tier classification.** The investigation found no clearly superior Minoan-period alternative for any of them.

2. **The Tier 3 classification is philosophically honest.** These names were borrowed from later Greek tradition because the concepts they encode -- structured state delegations, meta-catalogs, qualitative synthesis, abstract architectural patterns -- are intellectual achievements of the Classical and Hellenistic periods, not Bronze Age administrative practices.

3. **The Bronze Age had the practice but not the concept.** This is the most interesting finding: the Mycenaean administration *did* things that resemble what these names describe (they classified tablets, tracked deficits, organized distributed watchers) but they did not name these practices as abstract concepts. The naming gap between practice and concept is itself a marker of the intellectual shift from palatial administration to philosophical thinking.

4. **Doctrine enrichment opportunities exist.** While no migrations are warranted, the research surfaces Bronze Age operational precedents that could enrich the doctrine:
   - **Theoria section**: Note that *te-o* (the divine) is Tier 1, establishing that the observation is of something already named in Bronze Age
   - **Synkrisis section**: Cite *o-pe-ro*/*a-pu-do-si* as evidence that Mycenaean accounting practiced comparison even without naming it
   - **Argus Pattern section**: Cite the Pylos *o-ka* tablets as a Bronze Age operational precedent for distributed surveillance

5. **Argus is already Tier 2, not Tier 3.** The track spec misclassified it. The concordance is authoritative.
